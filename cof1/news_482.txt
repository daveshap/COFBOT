This article reports on a study that shows that businesses are slow to react to HIV/AIDS. They wait until a problem reaches a certain level before they even consider taking action to prevent it from getting worse. This is a classic example of a negative externality. A negative externality refers to the fact that a firm does not take into consideration the costs that their actions have on an individual or a group of people. In the case of HIV/AIDS, the firms have no incentive to help prevent the spread of AIDS among their employees because it does not affect their bottom line. It is not until enough of the employees are infected with HIV/AIDS that the firm will finally take action. The firms are also not motivated to help prevent the spread of the disease because of the stigma attached to the disease.
